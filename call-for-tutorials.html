Call · 01

Overview

The DAI 2026 tutorial program will provide accessible, high-quality educational sessions for researchers, students, engineers, and practitioners working on distributed artificial intelligence, multi-agent systems, and agentic AI.

Tutorials should help participants enter an important area, understand a mature body of work, learn a practical system or methodology, or connect DAI research with an external topic that can motivate new research and applications. We welcome both foundational tutorials and hands-on tutorials that teach tools, benchmarks, implementation patterns, evaluation methods, or deployment practice.

DAI 2026 especially encourages tutorials that match the conference theme, Agentic AI Goes Live: Science, Systems, and Societies, by linking rigorous concepts with usable methods and real systems.

Call · 02

Tutorial Objectives

DAI 2026 tutorials should serve one or more of the following objectives.

  • Introduce newcomers to major topics in DAI, multi-agent systems, and agentic AI.
  • Survey a mature area of DAI research or practice.
  • Explain an emerging area of high importance to the DAI community.
  • Teach practical tools, frameworks, benchmarks, or engineering patterns for distributed and agentic AI systems.
  • Connect DAI audiences with external topics that can benefit from, or contribute to, distributed AI.
  • Help expert non-specialists enter a DAI area without assuming deep prior exposure.

Call · 04

Suggested Themes

Tutorial proposals on any topic relevant to DAI are welcome. The following themes are especially encouraged for DAI 2026.

Practical LLM Agent Frameworks in 2026

  • Hands-on use of LangGraph, AutoGen, CrewAI, Smolagents, OpenAI Agents SDK, and related frameworks
  • Agent orchestration, memory, tool use, context engineering, evaluation, and production patterns

Foundations of MARL in the LLM Era

  • Core concepts in reinforcement learning and multi-agent reinforcement learning
  • Self-play, curriculum learning, credit assignment, communication, coordination, and transfer in modern agent systems

Mechanism Design Meets Large Language Models

  • Game theory, mechanism design, auctions, markets, contracts, social choice, and strategic behavior of AI agents
  • Agent economies, machine-payable APIs, incentive design, algorithmic collusion, and governance

Embodied Agents: From VLA Models to Real Robots

  • Vision-language-action models, world models, multi-robot coordination, and heterogeneous embodied teams
  • Sim-to-real transfer, safety layers, hardware-software co-design, and physical AI applications

Evaluating, Debugging, and Benchmarking Multi-Agent Systems

  • Benchmarks, reproducibility, observability, tracing, failure recovery, and AgentOps
  • Evaluation of safety, reliability, interpretability, emergent behavior, and human-agent collaboration

Call · 05

Proposal Content

Tutorial proposals should be submitted as a single PDF and should include the following information.

  • Tutorial title, proposed presenters, affiliations, contact information, and short bios.
  • A concise abstract, motivation, and statement of relevance to DAI 2026.
  • Target audience, assumed background, prerequisites, and expected learning outcomes.
  • A detailed outline of the tutorial, including timing for each major part.
  • Preferred delivery style: lecture, hands-on session, live demonstration, interactive exercise, or hybrid format.
  • Planned materials such as slides, notebooks, code repositories, datasets, reading lists, or software demonstrations.
  • Any technical requirements, including compute, installation, network access, or room setup needs.
  • Prior tutorial, teaching, conference, or community-building experience of the presenters.
  • Expected audience size and plan for making the tutorial accessible to participants with different backgrounds.

Call · 06

Review Criteria

Tutorial proposals will be reviewed by the DAI 2026 workshop and tutorial chairs, with additional community input when needed. Selection will consider relevance to DAI, timeliness, pedagogical value, presenter expertise, clarity of learning outcomes, expected audience interest, feasibility of the proposed format, balance between foundations and practice, and complementarity with other tutorials and workshops.

Strong proposals will make clear what participants will be able to understand or do by the end of the session. Tutorials that provide reusable materials, runnable examples, public repositories, benchmarks, or well-structured reading paths are especially encouraged.

Call · 07

Submission

Tutorial proposals should be prepared as a single PDF. The proposal deadline, notification date, and submission link will be posted on this page when the tutorial submission process is finalized.

Accepted tutorial presenters will be expected to provide final title, abstract, presenter information, and public materials in time for the DAI 2026 program update.
